Hot take:

It's okay to build things and be profitable.

It's okay to raise money and burn it.

The problem is the normalization of burning money for years to get to profitability.

If you raise, have a clear path to profitability. And go prove it.

Too many companies raise a seed round to build an MVP, raise an A to scale it, raise a B because the unit economics don't work, and then go on a podcast and talk about "we're building for the long term" when the reality is: they never had product market fit, they were just burning investor cash hoping that scaling would solve the problem.

Scaling doesn't solve the problem.

You just have a bigger burning pile of cash.

Most of these companies should have raised a smaller round, stayed small, and figured out if the product actually worked before scaling it.

But that's counter-cultural now.

The advice "stay small and profitable" is almost seen as cowardice.

So people raise more, burn more, and pray.

I raised $12M in 2015, was profitable in 2016, and it's the best decision I ever made.

Not because of the money, but because profitability forced us to build something people actually wanted to pay for.

If you can build something people will pay for, you don't need as much money as you think.

If you can't, you should know that early, not after you've burned $50M.

Stay small until you can't.

Then raise to scale what's already working.

That's not cowardice.

That's how you build something real.
