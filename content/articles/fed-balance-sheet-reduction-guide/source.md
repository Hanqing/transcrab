# A User's Guide to Reducing the Federal Reserve's Balance Sheet

**Authors**: Alyssa G. Anderson, Alessandro Barbarino, Anthony M. Diercks, Stephen Miran

**Date**: March 2026

## Abstract

For the avoidance of doubt: 1) This catalog presents and analyzes a variety of options for reducing the Federal Reserve's balance sheet. Nothing here is an endorsement of any specific policy option; this is a menu of options. Combined, we estimate these options open the door to balance sheet reduction of $1.2 to $2.1 trillion within the Fed's current ample reserves framework. While we do not advocate for or against a return to a scarce reserves regime, further reductions would be possible with a return to scarce reserves. 2) The process of materially shrinking the balance sheet would require a great deal of implementation and rulemaking work in advance and would take time, at least a year and quite possibly several, before the Fed can begin shrinking its balance sheet.

## Key Findings

This paper presents a suite of policy options for reducing the size of the Fed's balance sheet, many of which aim to reduce the demand for reserves and shift those boundaries down. Nothing here should be considered advocacy or endorsement of any particular option. This paper is a catalog, and future Federal Reserve leadership will decide which, if any, options to pursue.

### Policy Options Summary

The paper identifies 15 major policy options across several categories:

1. **Regulatory Changes** - Recognizing discount window capacity in liquidity coverage ratios, recalibrating requirements during stress periods, and reforming internal liquidity stress testing expectations.

2. **Supervisory Changes** - Equalizing supervisory treatment of Treasury bills and reserves, revising resolution liquidity requirements.

3. **Decrease Profitability of Holding Reserves** - Conducting policy with EFFR above IORB, implementing reserve tiering.

4. **Increase Attractiveness of Substitutes** - Expanding access to FIMA repo facility, addressing foreign bank organization reserve demand through swap lines.

5. **Reduce Precautionary Holdings** - Using communication to reduce stigma of Standing Repo Facility, extending SRP borrowing window.

6. **Reduce Non-Reserve Liabilities** - Recalibrating Treasury General Account management, discouraging use of foreign reverse repo pool.

### Estimated Impact

Using Monte Carlo simulations with 1,000,000 draws following OMB Circular A-4 standards:

- **Reserve demand reduction**: $825 billion to $1,750 billion
- **Total balance sheet reduction**: $1,150 billion to $2,125 billion (95% confidence interval)

## How We Got Here

### Federal Reserve Balance Sheet Structure

**Assets** (Total: $6.6 trillion):
- U.S. Treasury Securities: $4.4 trillion
- Agency Securities (MBS): $2.0 trillion
- Other Assets: $284 billion

**Liabilities** (Total: $6.6 trillion):
- Reserve Balances: $3.1 trillion
- Currency in Circulation: $2.4 trillion
- Treasury General Account: $806 billion
- Foreign Reverse Repo Pool: $325 billion
- Other Liabilities: $52 billion

### Evolution of Implementation Framework

**Three Reserve Regimes**:

1. **Scarce Reserves** (Pre-2008): Market on steep downward-sloping demand curve; frequent central bank intervention needed; high interest rate sensitivity.

2. **Ample Reserves** (Current): Market on modestly downward-sloping portion; minimal response to typical fluctuations but responsive to sharp moves; EFFR close to IORB.

3. **Abundant Reserves** (Post-pandemic): Market on flat demand curve; no interest rate sensitivity; EFFR below IORB.

### Historical Timeline

- **Pre-GFC**: Scarce reserves regime, balance sheet ~$900 billion (6% of GDP)
- **2008-2014**: Shift to abundant reserves through quantitative easing; balance sheet reached 15% of GDP
- **2017-2019**: First quantitative tightening; ended due to September 2019 money market stress
- **2020-2021**: Pandemic-era expansion; balance sheet reached 36% of GDP
- **2022-2025**: Quantitative tightening resumed; returned to ample reserves framework
- **Late 2025**: Treasury bill purchases resumed to maintain ample reserves

## The Concept of "Regulatory Dominance"

A key insight of this paper is that modern reserve demand is substantially driven by the regulatory environment. Banks are required by various regulations to hold certain levels of high-quality liquid assets (HQLA), which can include reserves or Treasury securities. The regulatory framework—both formal requirements and supervisory expectations—substantially amplifies baseline reserve demand.

This "regulatory dominance" view challenges the traditional assumption that the Fed cannot reduce its balance sheet materially. It recognizes that central bank policy can affect not only the supply of reserves but also demand for reserves through regulation and implementation policies.

## Implications

The paper argues that further balance sheet reduction is possible within an ample reserves framework by reducing demand for reserves through policy changes. This challenges the widespread assumption that further reduction requires a return to scarce reserves.

If undertaken, balance sheet reduction should proceed slowly and carefully to ensure financial markets can absorb the reissuance of securities rolling off the Fed's balance sheet.
