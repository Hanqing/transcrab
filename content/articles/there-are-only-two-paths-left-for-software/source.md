# There Are Only Two Paths Left for Software

*By Marc Andreessen*

The software industry has changed a lot since I wrote "[Why Software Is Eating the World](https://a16z.com/why-software-is-eating-the-world/)" in 2011. Most enterprise software has moved to the cloud. Open source has become the default approach for building new software stacks. Consumer smartphones are ubiquitous. SaaS has become the standard business model.

And yet — for most of the world, software adoption is still early. The opportunity ahead is enormous.

Looking forward, we see only two viable paths for software companies:

**Path 1: Win the infrastructure layer**

The cloud providers are dominating infrastructure. AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud have built massive businesses by providing the underlying compute, storage, and networking that software runs on. There's essentially an oligopoly at the infrastructure level, with AWS maintaining a leadership position despite intense competition.

The opportunity here is to compete with one of these three, or to build a new category of infrastructure software that the incumbents haven't yet addressed. The winner in infrastructure typically gets 40-60% gross margins and dominates for a decade or more. This is the high-stakes, winner-take-all path.

**Path 2: Win the applications layer**

The application layer is where software creates direct value for end users and businesses. This is where vertical SaaS lives — specialized software built for specific industries or workflows. Healthcare, construction, logistics, legal, accounting — every vertical is being transformed by software, and most are still early in that transformation.

The opportunity here is to build deeply integrated applications that understand a specific domain better than anyone else. The winning applications companies typically achieve 70-80% gross margins and build enormous moats through network effects, data, and deep customer relationships.

The key insight is that you don't have to choose between these paths, but you do have to choose. The companies that try to do both — build infrastructure AND applications — almost always fail to execute either well. Focus is everything.

Both paths require the same fundamentals: a compelling product vision, exceptional engineering, and the ability to acquire and retain customers at scale. But the go-to-market strategies differ significantly. Infrastructure companies sell to engineers and CTOs. Application companies sell to business users and domain experts.

The next decade of software will be defined by which companies can execute on one of these two paths. The middle ground — trying to be a generalist in an increasingly specialized world — is disappearing.

Choose your path. Execute relentlessly. And good luck.
